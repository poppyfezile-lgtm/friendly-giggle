# Brand + Website Onboarding Questions

## Purpose

Use this Markdown file to collect brand answers before creating any brand-specific output.

This file is a **generic factory intake asset**. It does not contain a completed brand profile, website plan, campaign, carousel, caption, CTA, or finished brand folder.

## Operating Rules

- Ask **one question at a time** unless the user explicitly requests bulk intake.
- Collect the 9 core brand fields before any story discovery, slide planning, caption writing, CTA selection, visual generation, sitemap planning, or website copywriting.
- Use the website-specific questions only when the user wants to make, rebuild, audit, or brief a website for a brand.
- If an answer is vague, ask one short follow-up before moving on.
- Restate each cleaned answer in the requested format before asking the next question.
- Do not personalize this file for one brand. Put brand-specific answers in the separate brand folder or website project folder.

---

# Part 1 - Core Brand Intake Questions

These 9 questions are required for every brand run.

## 1. Brand Name

**Question**  
What exact brand name should appear in on-image text, captions, CTA language, and website copy?

**Why this matters**  
This is the public-facing name the system must preserve exactly.

**Examples**
- A company name
- A personal brand name
- A product name
- A project name

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Use the working project name for now.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Brand name: <exact public-facing name>`

---

## 2. Audience

**Question**  
Who is the primary audience this brand wants to reach first?

**Why this matters**  
This identifies who the content and website should speak to before anyone else.

**Examples**
- Founders adopting AI tools
- Educators tracking AI in classrooms
- Executives buying AI training
- Creators improving workflow with AI

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Professionals interested in practical AI.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Audience: <primary audience sentence>`

---

## 3. Offer

**Question**  
What does the brand sell, deliver, teach, or want people to do?

**Why this matters**  
This defines the brand's practical value and the likely conversion path.

**Examples**
- AI workshops
- Consulting calls
- Software demos
- Newsletter subscriptions

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Practical AI guidance and resources for the target audience.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Offer: <clear offer sentence>`

---

## 4. Tone

**Question**  
What tone should the brand use?

**Why this matters**  
This controls how the brand sounds across content, website copy, captions, and CTAs.

**Examples**
- Clear and practical
- Rigorous and evidence-first
- Warm and accessible
- Bold and founder-friendly

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Clear, practical, and trustworthy.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Tone: <3 to 5 tone descriptors>`

---

## 5. Visual Style

**Question**  
What should the visuals feel like?

**Why this matters**  
This controls the look of slides, graphics, website sections, image prompts, and page layouts.

**Examples**
- Clean editorial
- Minimal technical
- Bold startup style
- Premium executive style

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Clean editorial visuals with strong contrast and simple layouts.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Visual style: <visual style sentence>`

---

## 6. Goals

**Question**  
What should the brand content or website achieve first?

**Why this matters**  
This identifies the main business or communication outcome.

**Examples**
- Build authority
- Generate leads
- Drive website visits
- Grow newsletter signups

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Build authority and generate qualified interest.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Goals: <ranked or primary goal statement>`

---

## 7. Content Themes

**Question**  
What recurring AI-related themes should the system cover?

**Why this matters**  
These are the story buckets the system should return to over time.

**Examples**
- AI tools for operations
- AI policy and risk
- AI in education
- Sector adoption of AI

**Rough-answer fallback**  
AI tools, AI policy, AI in education, and the brand's sector plus AI.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Content themes: <theme list>`

---

## 8. Platforms

**Question**  
Where will this content be published first?

**Why this matters**  
The platform affects caption length, format, CTA style, slide density, and website traffic goals.

**Examples**
- LinkedIn
- X
- Instagram
- Newsletter
- Website blog

**Rough-answer fallback**  
LinkedIn as the primary platform.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Platforms: <primary platform plus optional secondary platforms>`

---

## 9. Constraints

**Question**  
What must the system avoid or enforce?

**Why this matters**  
These are trust, safety, compliance, tone, or brand-protection rules.

**Examples**
- No hype
- No legal advice
- No exaggerated ROI claims
- No competitor attacks

**Rough-answer fallback**  
No hype, no unsupported claims, no confusing jargon, and conservative claims only.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Constraints: <constraint list>`

# Part 2 - Website-Specific Intake Questions

Use these only when the user wants to create, redesign, brief, audit, or launch a website for the brand.

## Minimum Website Intake

These questions are usually needed before making a useful website strategy, sitemap, wireframe, homepage draft, landing page, or developer brief.

## 10. Website Purpose

**Question**  
What is the main job of the website?

**Why this matters**  
A website built to generate leads should be structured differently from a website built for credibility, recruiting, ecommerce, investor trust, or content publishing.

**Examples**
- Generate qualified leads
- Explain the offer clearly
- Sell products
- Book calls
- Build credibility
- Publish thought leadership

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Make the brand credible and convert qualified visitors into leads.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Website purpose: <primary website job>`

---

## 11. Primary Website CTA

**Question**  
What is the one main action visitors should take on the website?

**Why this matters**  
The main CTA controls page hierarchy, navigation, buttons, forms, and homepage structure.

**Examples**
- Book a call
- Request a demo
- Buy now
- Join the newsletter
- Download a guide
- Apply now

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Book a call or request more information.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Primary website CTA: <single main action>`

---

## 12. Secondary Website CTAs

**Question**  
What secondary actions should visitors be able to take?

**Why this matters**  
Secondary CTAs help visitors who are interested but not ready for the main conversion.

**Examples**
- Read case studies
- View pricing
- Watch a demo
- Subscribe to updates
- Download a resource
- Contact support

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Read more proof, join the newsletter, or contact the brand.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Secondary website CTAs: <secondary action list>`

---

## 13. Required Pages

**Question**  
Which pages must exist at launch?

**Why this matters**  
This defines the first sitemap and prevents unnecessary page sprawl.

**Examples**
- Home
- About
- Services
- Pricing
- Case studies
- Blog
- Contact
- FAQ

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Home, About, Offer or Services, Proof, and Contact.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Required pages: <launch page list>`

---

## 14. Offer or Service Structure

**Question**  
How should the website present the brand's offers, services, products, or packages?

**Why this matters**  
The website needs a clear way to explain what is available, who it is for, and what action to take next.

**Examples**
- One main service page
- Separate pages for each service
- Product collection pages
- Tiered packages
- Custom quote flow

**Rough-answer fallback**  
Use one clear offer page with sections for outcomes, process, proof, and CTA.

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Offer structure: <how offers should be presented>`

---

## 15. Visitor Segments

**Question**

**Why this matters**  
A site for multiple audiences may need segmented navigation, landing pages, or homepage sections.

**Examples**

**Cleaned answer format**  
`Visitor objections: <objection list>`

# Part 5 - Website Intake Done Criteria

Website intake is complete when the operator can state:

- Who the website is for
- What the website must achieve
- What the primary CTA is
- Which pages are required at launch
- What offers or products must be explained
- What proof and trust assets are available
- What design assets or style rules exist
- What functionality and integrations are needed
- What legal, accessibility, privacy, or compliance limits apply
- What platform, editing, analytics, launch, and maintenance constraints exist

Do not create the sitemap, homepage copy, wireframes, or website build brief until the needed website intake fields are collected.
